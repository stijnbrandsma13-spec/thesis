\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

In many econometric models it is assumed that the aggregate behaves the same as the individual up to an error term.
For example, a model for wage might specify that ones wage is expected to increase by a parameter $\beta$ with one extra year of schooling, where $\beta$ is the same for each individual.
However, for some situations this assumption can be incorrect.
The probability of buying a car increases when you paint it pink for some people, whereas for some people it will decrease.
In this case it makes sense to model this heterogeneity in preferences.

An example of models that incorporate this heterogeneity are called random coefficient models (RCMs).
These models are widely used in many fields such as labor economics, industrial organization, and asset pricing \citep[e.g.][]{BerryLevinsohnPakes1995, CarneiroHeckmanVytlacil2011, BelzilHansen2007, KoijenYogo2019}.
An extensive overview of RCMs in panel data is given by \citet{HsiaoPesaran2008}.
A specific example of RCMs are random coefficient discrete choice models (RCDCMs).
These often extend discrete choice models such as probit, logit and the multinomial logit \citep{CameronTrivedi2005}, see for example the mixed logit \citep[Chap.~6]{Train2009}.

The mixed logit (MXL) is exactly the same as the multinomial logit (MNL), with the exception that the coefficients are random.
One advantage of MXL over the MNL is the lack of the IIA (independence of irrelevant alternatives) assumption \citep{Train2009}.
The mixed logit is applied in many studies \citep[e.g.][]{NicoletNegenbornAtasoy2022,YinCherchi2024,ZhouWangYangGanLiuZi2025}.
When the mixing distribution of random coefficients is discrete, MXL becomes a latent class model, specifically the latent class multinomial logit (LC-MNL) \citep{Train2009}, as used in \citet{WangYangSongSia2014}, and \citet{LiGaoYangLiu2022}.
LC-MNL will be the model of interest in this thesis.

Many estimation procedures have been proposed for the LC-MNL. 
\citet{Train2009} suggests a simulated maximum likelihood approach, as well as an expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm and a hierarchical Bayes method with Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC).
\citet{Train2008} dives into the EM approach. \citet{BurdaHardingHausman2008} and \citet{Rossi2012} provide more on the hierarchical Bayes method.
The literature has also used simulated method of moments \citep{McFadden1989} and minimum distance \citep{BeranMillar1994}.
\citet{FoxKimRyanBajari2011} experencied problems to some degree with all the methods above.
Therefore, they proposed a computationally simple estimator (commonly called the FKRB estimator).
This estimator has been proven to be attractive to use in the recent literature \citep[e.g.][]{HoudeMyers2019,BlundellGowrisankaranLanger2020}.

The key part to the approach of \citet{FoxKimRyanBajari2011} is that the LC-MNL can be rewritten such that the parameters enter linearly.
Therefore, the parameters can be estimated with OLS. 
However, the parameters have to abide to certain constraints.
\citet{HeissHetzeneckerOsterhaus2022} show that these constraints make the FKRB estimator equivalent to a nonnegative LASSO (NNL) estimator.
With this insight they extend the estimator to a nonnegative elastic net estimator.

Another attempt of an improvement to the FKRB estimator has been done by \citet{Train2016}.
He approximates the distribution of random coefficients with splines, polynomials or step functions, instead of with a discrete mixture as done by \citet{FoxKimRyanBajari2011}.
This approach can decrease the dimensionality of the model significantly.
Strictly speaking, this estimator is not estimating a LC-MNL anymore, since the distribution is not discrete here. 
This estimator is still comparable to the others, as the other estimators are also used in a context where the researcher does not have the belief that the coefficients have a discrete distribution, but a continuous distribution. 
In these cases the researcher chooses discrete mixture estimators for there computational properties.
When the support of the discrete distribution becomes sufficiently dense, this distribution can approximate any distribution arbitrarily close \citep{McFaddenTrain2000}.

Of course, in most of the cases, the researcher wants to conduct inference. 
\citet{FoxKimRyanBajari2011} show multiple methods of constructing confidence intervals for their estimator.
They first propose clipping the intervals generated from clustered heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors, from the unconstrained OLS procedure, inside the $[0,1]$ interval.
However, though this produces correct coverage,\footnote{We speak of correct coverage when, under the null, the probability of the estimator belonging to the $\alpha$\%-confidence interval is more than or equal to $\alpha$.} the confidence interval is observed to be quite conservative, in the sense that the confidence interval covers more than it has to.
Then they discuss resampling methods such as the standard bootstrap \citep[see][]{Efron1979} or the $m$-out-of-$n$ bootstrap.
They find, however, little evidence that this produces correct coverage.
Lastly, they consider Tikhonov regularization.
They do observe a slight improvement in that approach.
This thesis will explore other forms of regularization, to see if there is an improvement, in the sense that the confidence intervals might shrink closer to their exact correct coverage.

The natural paradigm for regularization for this estimator lies in the theory of penalized sieve minimum distance estimators (PSMDs).
As also recognized by \citet{HeissHetzeneckerOsterhaus2022}, the FKRB estimator is a specific example of a sieve estimator.
This thesis will also explicitly show the connection between the FKRB estimator and PSMD estimators.

Sieves have first been mentioned by \citet{Grenander1981}.
A sieve space is a space of approximations of sets of a bigger set, where the approximations become dense in the limit.
Many papers have used some form of sieve estimation, including \citet{GallantNychka1987}, who proposed an estimator that can be written as a sieve maximum likelihood estimator; \citet{WhiteWooldridge1991} and \citet{ChenShen1998}, who developed theory on sieve estimation in the context of dependent data; and \citet{Andrews1991} and \citet{Newey1997}, who developed much of the asymptotic theory on series estimators.

An extensive overview of sieve estimation is given by \citet{Chen2007}.
\citet{AiChen2003} and \citet{NeweyPowell2003} laid the groundwork for PSMD estimators by describing the sieve minimum distance (SMD) estimator.
This was extended to PSMD estimators by \citet{ChenPouzo2012} by adding a penalty term, which can be seen as a regularization term.
The usefulness of such estimators in inference for nonparametric instrumental variable (NPIV) models has been illustrated by \citet{ChenPouzo2015}.
