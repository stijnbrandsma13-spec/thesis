\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Many econometric models of economic behavior allow for heterogeneity in individual responses to explanatory variables, which is often only captured in the error term.
Random coefficient models provide a more flexible and structural framework to capture this heterogeneity, allowing parameters to vary across agents according to an unknown distribution.
An extensive overview of random coefficient models is given by \citet{HsiaoPesaran2008}.
These models are widely used in labor economics, industrial organization, and asset pricing \citep[e.g.][]{BerryLevinsohnPakes1995, CarneiroHeckmanVytlacil2011, BelzilHansen2007, KoijenYogo2019}.

In these models, we cannot estimate deterministic coefficients because they are inherently random.
Instead, we estimate the distribution of the random coefficients to gain insight into the structure of the heterogeneity in the data.
While a parametric approach—assuming the distribution belongs to a specific family identified by a finite-dimensional parameter—is common, it is only justified if one has a strong reason to believe the true distribution belongs to that chosen family.
When no such strong prior belief exists, a nonparametric approach might be a solution.
This approach is less restrictive, as it often imposes only mild assumptions on the distribution.
Nonparametric estimators often perfrom some optimization over a large function space.
However, to remain computationally feasible, these estimators sometimes restrict this space to a smaller, more manageable subspace.

Sieve estimators are an example of such estimators. 
They perform the optimization in a smaller function space.
The size of this space is determined by the sample size.
As the sample size increases, the function space grows, beccoming dense in the larger function space in the limit.
This approach has been suggested by \citet{Grenander1981}, who called the sequence of smaller function spaces generated by the sample size ``sieves''.
After the introduction of sieve estimation by \citet{Grenander1981}, many papers on sieve estimation have followed, including \citet{GallantNychka1987}, who proposed an estimator that can be written as a sieve maximum likelihood estimator; \citet{WhiteWooldridge1991} and \citet{ChenShen1998}, who developed theory on sieve estimation in the context of dependent data; and \citet{Andrews1991} and \citet{Newey1997}, who developed much of the asymptotic theory on series estimators.

An extensive overview of sieve estimation is given by \citet{Chen2007}.
This overview includes many examples of useful sieve classes, of which the class of Hermite polynomials spaces seems to be the most relevant to this thesis, as it allows for the estimation of distributions defined over the whole real line.
If a random coefficient has a smaller support, other sieve classes might be more appropriate.
Furthermore, more creative nonlinear sieve classes, such as the class of neural network spaces, might be interesting to explore.

Building on this foundation, \citet{AiChen2003} and \citet{NeweyPowell2003} laid the groundwork for the PSMD estimator by proposing a sieve minimum distance (SMD) estimator.
This was extended to the PSMD estimator by \citet{ChenPouzo2012} by adding a penalty term, and its usefulness in nonparametric instrumental variable (NPIV) models was illustrated by \citet{ChenPouzo2015}.

This thesis will apply the PSMD estimator to random coefficient models.
Many estimators have already been developed, tested and applied in this context in the literature.
For example, \citet{FoxKimRyanBajari2011} propose a simple nonparametric estimator for random coefficient models.
