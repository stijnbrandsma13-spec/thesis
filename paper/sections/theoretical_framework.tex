\section{Theoretical Framework}
\label{sec:theory}
\subsection{Mixed logit}
\label{sec:mxl}
We start by introducing the mixed logit model in detail.
This is the model of interest for this thesis.
The MXL can be derived from various behaviour specifications \citep{Train2009}.
We will show the derivation from a random coefficient discrete choice model.

In the model we assume that individual $i$, where $i = 1,\ldots,N$, makes a choice $j$ between $J$ alternatives and one outside good ($j=0$).
We model the latent utility $u_{ij}\in\mathbb{R}$ for individual $i$ for choice $j$ as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mxllatentutil}
    u_{ij} = \bm{x}_{ij}^\prime \bm{\beta}_{i} + \varepsilon_{ij},
\end{equation}
where $\bm{x}_{ij} \in \mathbb{R}^K$ denotes the column vector with observed exogenous variables for individual $i$, choice $j$.
Furthermore, $\bm{\beta}_{i}\in \mathbb{R}^K$ denotes the unobserved column vector of individual-specific random coefficients, for individual $i$. 
The random coefficient vectors are are independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) draws from a cumulative distribution function $F_0$ with support $\mathcal{B}$.\footnote{This means that for each individual $i$, the random coefficient vector $\bm{\beta}_{i}$ as a whole is jointly distributed as $F_0$. This does not mean that each element of a random coefficient vector is marginally distributed as $F_0$.}
When the random coefficients have finite support (i.e. $\mathcal{B}$ has finite cardinality) we speak of a latent class model \citep{Train2009}.
Specifically, we call then call this the latent class multinomial logit model (LC-MNL).
The distribution function is not observed.
Next, $\varepsilon_{ij}\in \mathbb{R}$ denotes the unobserved idiosyncratic error term for individual $i$, choice $j$. 
The error terms are i.i.d. draws from a Type I extreme value distribution.
Also, we assume strict exogeneity of the observed variables and independence of the unobserved components
That is, $\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_{ij}\mid \bm{x}_{i1}, \ldots,\bm{x}_{iJ}] = 0$ and $\bm{\beta}_i \perp \varepsilon_{ij}$ for all $i,j$.
At last, for $j=0$ (the outside good) we impose $u_{i0} = \varepsilon_{i0}$.

Let $y_{ij}$ denote the indicator of the event that individual $i$ chooses choice $j$. 
We assume individuals maximize utility. Then this implies
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mxlydef}
    y_{ij} = \mathds{1}\{u_{ij} > u_{ik}, \text{ for all } j \neq k\} = \prod_{\substack{k=1; \\ k\neq j}}^{J}\mathds{1}\{u_{ij} > u_{ik}\}.
\end{equation}

A useful object of interest is the probability of $y_{ij} = 1$ conditional on $\bm{x}_i$, where we denote $\bm{x}_i = (\bm{x}_{i1}^\prime,\ldots,\bm{x}_{iJ}^\prime)$ as the stacked row vector of the exogenous variables for individual $i$.
Define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mxlgdef}
    g_j(\bm{x}_{i},\bm{\beta}_{i}) \coloneq \mathbb{P}(y_{ij} = 1 \mid \bm{x}_i, \bm{\beta}_i).
\end{equation}
We can derive an analytical expression \citep[see][Chap.~3]{Maddala1983} in the form of
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mxlganalytical}
    g_j(\bm{x}_{i},\bm{\beta}_{i}) = \frac{\exp{(\bm{x}_{ij}^\prime \bm{\beta}_i)}}{1 + \sum_{k=1}^{J}\exp{(\bm{x}_{ik}^\prime \bm{\beta}_i)}}.
\end{equation}
The only difference with \citet{Maddala1983} is that $\bm{\beta}_i$ is stochastic in our model. 
However, since we are also conditioning on $\bm{\beta}_i$, the derivation remains the same.
It then follows that
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mxlcondprob}
    \mathbb{P}(y_{ij} = 1 \mid \bm{x}_{i}) = \int_{\mathcal{B}} g_j(\bm{x}_{i}, \bm{\beta}) \, \mathrm{d}F_{0}(\bm{\beta}).
\end{equation}

\subsection{FKRB Estimator}
\label{sec:FKRBestimator}
A commonly used estimator for MXL is the estimator proposed by \citet{FoxKimRyanBajari2011}, which is hereafter referred to as the FKRB estimator.
They approximate the distribution of random coefficients by a finite mixture over fixed support points.
Next they rewrite \eqref{eq:mxlcondprob} such that the parameters of interest enter the equation linearly.
Then they show that these parameters can be consistently estimated by means of (constrained) least squares.

\subsubsection{Derivation}
\label{sec:FKRBderivation}
For completeness, we first show the derivation of the estimator. 
For some $R \in \mathbb{N}_+$, let $\tilde{\mathcal{B}} = \{\bm{\beta}^{(1)}, \bm{\beta}^{(2)}, \ldots, \bm{\beta}^{(R)}\}$.
The $\bm{\beta}^{(r)}$ should not be parameters of the model; they should be prespecified points. 
We let $\theta_r$ denote the value of the probability mass function of $\bm{\beta}_{i}$ at $\bm{\beta}^{(r)}$, i.e. $\theta_r = \mathbb{P}(\bm{\beta}_{i} = \bm{\beta}^{(r)})$.
Then under the assumption $\tilde{\mathcal{B}} = \mathcal{B}$ we have that \eqref{eq:mxlcondprob} boils down to
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FKRBpij}
    \mathbb{P}(y_{ij} = 1 \mid \bm{x}_{ij}) = \sum_{r = 1}^{R} \theta_r g_j\! \left(\bm{x_i},\bm{\beta}^{(r)}\right).
\end{equation}
We can then add $y_{ij}$ to both sides and move $\mathbb{P}(y_{ij}=1\mid \bm{x}_{ij})$ to the right-hand side:
\begin{align}
\label{eq:FKRBreg}
    y_{ij} &= \sum_{r = 1}^{R} \theta_r g_j\! \left(\bm{x_i},\bm{\beta}^{(r)}\right) + \left(y_{ij} - \mathbb{P}(y_{ij} = 1 \mid \bm{x}_{i})\right) \\
        \label{eq:FKRBzetadef}
        &= \sum_{r = 1}^{R} \theta_r z_{ijr} + \zeta_{ij} = \bm{z}_{ij}^\prime \bm{\theta} + \zeta_{ij},
\end{align}
where $\bm{\theta}=(\theta_1,\theta_2,\ldots,\theta_R)$, $\bm{z}_{ij} = (z_{ij1}, z_{ij2}, \ldots, z_{ijR})$, with $z_{ijr} = g_j(\bm{x}_{i}, \bm{\beta}^{(r)})$ and $\zeta_{ij} = y_{ij} - \mathbb{P}(y_{ij} = 1 \mid \bm{x}_{i})$.
We notice that $\mathbb{E}[\zeta_{ij}\mid \bm{z}_{ij}] = 0$. Therefore, the OLS estimator
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FKRBOLSest}
    \hat{\bm{\theta}}^\mathrm{OLS} = \argmin_{\bm{\theta}}{\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{j=1}^{J}(y_{ij}-\bm{z}^\prime_{ij}\bm{\theta})^2} = (\bm{Z}'\bm{Z})^{-1}\bm{Z}'\bm{y},
\end{equation}
where $\bm{y} = (y_{11}, y_{12}, \ldots, y_{1J}, y_{21}, y_{22}, \ldots, y_{2J}, \ldots, y_{N1},\ldots, y_{NJ})$ and
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FKRBZdef}
    \bm{Z} = \begin{pmatrix}
    z_{111} & \cdots & z_{1J1} & z_{211} & \cdots & z_{2J1} & \cdots & z_{N11} & \cdots & z_{NJ1} \\
    z_{112} & \cdots & z_{1J2} & z_{212} & \cdots & z_{2J2} & \cdots & z_{N12} & \cdots & z_{NJ2} \\
    \vdots  &        & \vdots  & \vdots  &        & \vdots  &        & \vdots  &        & \vdots  \\
    z_{11R} & \cdots & z_{1JR} & z_{21R} & \cdots & z_{2JR} & \cdots & z_{N1R} & \cdots & z_{NJR}
    \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
is a consistent estimator for $\bm{\theta}$ \citep{FoxKimRyanBajari2011}.

However, we know $\bm{\theta}$ resembles a probability measure. 
This gives the following constraints:
\begin{align}
\label{eq:FKRBconstraint1}
    &\sum_{r=1}^{R}\theta_r = 1, \\
\label{eq:FKRBconstraint2}
    &\theta_r \geq 0, \text{ for all } r = 1,\ldots,R.
\end{align}
In practice one often wants to abide to these constraints. 
Therefore, \citet{FoxKimRyanBajari2011} suggest the following constrained least squares estimator
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FKRBCLSest}
    \begin{split}
        \hat{\bm{\theta}}^{\mathrm{FKRB}} = &\argmin_{\bm{\theta}}{\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{j=1}^{J}(y_{ij}-\bm{z}^\prime_{ij}\bm{\theta})^2}, \\
            &\text{ subject to \eqref{eq:FKRBconstraint1} and \eqref{eq:FKRBconstraint2}}.
    \end{split}
\end{equation}
Under some additional regularity conditions, this estimator is also consistent \citep{Liew1976}.
One then tries to reconstruct $F_{0}$ according to
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FKRBest}
    \hat{F}_{0}^{\mathrm{FKRB}}(\bm{b}) = \sum_{r=1}^R \left[\hat{\theta}^{\mathrm{FKRB}}_r \prod_{i=1}^{K}\mathds{1}{\{\beta^{r}_i < b_i\}}\right].
\end{equation}
We call this the FKRB estimator.\footnote{In the literature, \eqref{eq:FKRBCLSest} and \eqref{eq:FKRBest} are interchangeably called the FKRB estimator.}
\subsubsection{Inference}
After having done the estimation procedure, the researcher often wants to conduct inference on $\hat{\bm{\theta}}^{\mathrm{FKRB}}$.
Because of the nonparametric nature of the estimator, it is hard to derive a finite-sample distribution of the estimator.
For many estimators, the central limit theorem can do a lot of heavy lifting in establishing at least the asymptotic distribution, which is then often Gaussian.
However, when the true parameter is on the boundary, asymptotic normality breaks down \citep{Andrews1999}.
\citet{Andrews1999,Andrews2001,Andrews2002} give the asymptotic distribution for related cases with a true parameter on the boundary.
\citet{AndrewsGuggenberger2010Applications} also propose a subsampling procedure for a related case.
These results however are unfortunately not general enough to also apply to the FKRB estimator \citep{FoxKimRyanBajari2011}.

\citet{FoxKimRyanBajari2011} have suggested an alternative approach.
They construct an initial confidence interval $\mathrm{CI}^{\mathrm{OLS}}_{\alpha}(\hat{\theta}^{(r)}_{\mathrm{FKRB}})$ at the $\alpha$ significance level for $\hat{\theta}^{(r)}_{\mathrm{FKRB}}$ with heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors that arise from the unconstrained OLS estimator $\hat{\bm{\theta}}^{\mathrm{OLS}}$ from \eqref{eq:FKRBOLSest}.
The need of heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors comes from the inherent heteroskedasticity introduced by the construction of the error term $\zeta_{ij}$ in \eqref{eq:FKRBzetadef}.
There are also clear clusters (each individual has one), so they also cluster the standard errors at the level of ``regression observations'' $j = 1,\ldots,J$ for each individual $i$.
More formally we have
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:CIOLS}
    \mathrm{CI}^{\mathrm{OLS}}_{\alpha}\left(\hat{\theta}^{(r)}_{\mathrm{FKRB}}\right) = \left[\hat{\theta}^{(r)}_{\mathrm{FKRB}} - z_{1-\frac{\alpha}{2}}\mathrm{SE}\left(\hat{\theta}^{(r)}_{\mathrm{OLS}}\right),\hat{\theta}^{(r)}_{\mathrm{FKRB}} + z_{1-\frac{\alpha}{2}}\mathrm{SE}\left(\hat{\theta}^{(r)}_{\mathrm{OLS}}\right)\right],
\end{equation}
where $z_{1-\frac{\alpha}{2}} = \Phi^{-1}(1-\frac{\alpha}{2})$ denotes the $1-\frac{\alpha}{2}$ quantile of the standard normal distribution and $\mathrm{SE}(\hat{\theta}^{(r)}_{\mathrm{OLS}})$ denotes the clustered heteroskedasticity-robust standard error of $\hat{\theta}^{(r)}_{\mathrm{OLS}}$.
Note that this acts as if the estimator is asymptotically normally distributed. 
Although this is wrong, it still remains the best approximation we currently have.
The only caveat is that this allows for confidence intervals that cross outside the $[0,1]$ region, while this is clearly not possible for the real parameters as illustrated by \eqref{eq:FKRBconstraint1} and \eqref{eq:FKRBconstraint2}.
The final interval that \citet{FoxKimRyanBajari2011} arrive at is therefore clipped to this region, i.e. 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:CIFKRB}
    \mathrm{CI}^{\mathrm{FKRB}}_{\alpha}\left(\hat{\theta}^{(r)}_{\mathrm{FKRB}}\right) = [0,1] \cap \mathrm{CI}^{\mathrm{OLS}}_{\alpha}\left(\hat{\theta}^{(r)}_{\mathrm{FKRB}}\right).
\end{equation}

We show that the FKRB estimator is also a special case of the PSMD estimator from \citet{ChenPouzo2012}.
Let
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:PSMDrho}
    \rho_{ij}(\bm{y},\bm{x};h) = y_{ij} - \int_{\mathcal{B}}g_j(\bm{x}_i,\bm{\beta})h(\bm{\beta})\,\mathrm{d}\bm{\beta},
\end{equation}
It follows that
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:PSMDexprho}
     \mathbb{E}[\rho_{ij}(\bm{y},\bm{x};h_0)\mid \bm{x}] = \mathbb{E}[y_{ij}\mid \bm{x}] - \int_{\mathcal{B}}g_j(\bm{x}_i,\bm{\beta})h_0(\bm{\beta})\,\mathrm{d}\bm{\beta} = 0
\end{equation}
for all $i,j$, under the identification $h_0(b) = \frac{\mathrm{d}F_{\bm{\beta}}(b)}{\mathrm{d}b}$, since
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:PSMDexpplink}
    \mathbb{E}[y_{ij}\mid \bm{x}] = \mathbb{E}[y_{ij}\mid \bm{x}_i] = \mathbb{P}(y_{ij}=1\mid \bm{x}_i) = \int_{\mathcal{B}}g_j(\bm{x}_{i},\bm{\beta})\,\mathrm{d}F_{\bm{\beta}} = \int_{\mathcal{B}}g_j(\bm{x}_i,\bm{\beta})h_0(\bm{\beta})\,\mathrm{d}\bm{\beta},
\end{equation}
by Proposition \ref{prop:rcdcmcondprop}.
This is exactly the framework in which \citet{ChenPouzo2012} provided the PSMD estimator for estimating $h_0$.

Define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:PSMDmdef}
    m_{ij}(\bm{x};h) \coloneq \mathbb{E}[\rho_{ij}(\bm{y},\bm{x};h)\mid \bm{x}].
\end{equation}
