\section{Theoretical Framework}
\label{sec:theory}

\subsection{Random Coefficient Models}
\label{sec:rcm}
Random coefficient models (RCMs) are a broad class of econometric models that explicitly model heterogeneity of agents by allowing coefficients to vary across individuals.
In the most general case one might present random coefficient models as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rcmgeneral}
    \bm{y}_{it} = f(\bm{x}_{it},\bm{\beta}_{it},\bm{\varepsilon}_{it}),
\end{equation}
where $f$ denotes a prespecified function. 
Furthermore, for individual $i$ at time $t$ we have that $\bm{y}_{it} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_y}$ denotes the observed vector of dependent variables, $\bm{x}_{it} \in \mathbb{R}^K$ denotes the observed vector of regressors, $\bm{\beta}_{it} \in \mathbb{R}^{K}$ denotes the unobserved vector of random coefficients, with unobserved distribution $\bm{\beta}_{it} \sim F_{\bm{\beta}_{it}}$, and $\bm{\varepsilon}_{it} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_y}$ denotes the unobserved vector of errors, with $\bm{\varepsilon}_{it} \sim F_{\bm{\varepsilon}_i}$.

One should notice that we always need more structure on the distribution of $\bm{\beta}_{it}$, as the most general case presented above leads to unidentifiability.
Therefore, all useful RCMs impose $\bm{\beta}_{it} \sim F_{\bm{\beta}}$ for all $i,t$.
Moreover, in most models we have $d_y = 1$.

\subsubsection{Random coefficient discrete choice models}
\label{sec:rcdcm}
A common example of RCMs are random coefficient dicrete choice models (RCDCMs).
This is also the model of interest in this thesis.
In the model we assume that individual $i$, where $i = 1,\ldots,N$, makes a choice between $J$ alternatives.
We model the latent utility $u_{ij}$ for individual $i$ for choice $j$ as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rcdcmlatent}
    u_{ij} = \bm{x}_{ij}^\prime \bm{\beta}_{ij} + \varepsilon_{ij},
\end{equation}
where $\bm{\beta}_{ij} \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} F_{\bm{\beta}}$ with support $\mathcal{B}$ and $\varepsilon_{ij} \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} F_\varepsilon$.
Let $y_{ij}$ denote the indicator of the event that individual $i$ chooses choice $j$. 
If we assume individuals maximize utility this implies
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rcdcmydef}
    y_{ij} = \mathds{1}\{u_{ij} > u_{ik}, \text{ for all } j \neq k\} = \prod_{\substack{k=1; \\ k\neq j}}^{J}\mathds{1}\{u_{ij} > u_{ik}\}.
\end{equation}
A useful object of interest is the probability of $y_{ij} = 1$ conditional on $x_i$.
\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:rcdcmcondprop}
    Consider the RCDCM as defined in Section \ref{sec:rcdcm}. 
    Let
    \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:rcdcmgdef}
        g_j(\bm{x}_{i},\bm{\beta}_{i}) \coloneq \mathbb{P}(y_{ij} = 1 \mid \bm{x}_i, \bm{\beta}_i) = \prod_{\substack{k=1;\\k\neq j}}^{J}F_v(\bm{x}_{ij}^\prime \bm{\beta}_{ij}-\bm{x}_{ik}^\prime \bm{\beta}_{ik}),
    \end{equation}
    with $\bm{x}_{i} = (\bm{x}_{i1},\ldots,\bm{x}_{iJ})$ and
    \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:rcdcmvdistribution}
        F_v(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}F_\varepsilon(x+t)\,\mathrm{d}F_\varepsilon(t).
    \end{equation}
    It follows that
    \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:rcdcmconditionalprobability}
        \mathbb{P}(y_{ij} = 1 \mid \bm{x}_{i}) = \int_{\mathcal{B}} g_j(\bm{x}_{i}, \bm{\beta}) \, \mathrm{d}F_{\bm{\beta}}.
    \end{equation}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
    See Appendix~\ref{appendix:rcdcm_probability}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Common Estimators}
\label{sec:alternatives}
\subsubsection{FKRB Estimator}
A commonly used estimator for RCDCMs is the estimator proposed by \citet{FoxKimRyanBajari2011}, which is hereafter referred to as the FKRB estimator.
They approximate the distribution of random coefficients by a finite mixture over fixed support points.
Next they rewrite \eqref{eq:rcdcmconditionalprobability} such that the parameters of interest enter the equation linearly.
Then they show that these parameters can be consistently estimated by means of (constrained) least squares.

For some $R \in \mathbb{N}_+$, let $\tilde{\mathcal{B}} = \{\bm{\beta}^{(1)}, \bm{\beta}^{(2)}, \ldots, \bm{\beta}^{(R)}\}$.
The $\bm{\beta}^{(r)}$ should not be parameters of the model; they should be prespecified points. 
We let $\theta_r$ denote the value of the probability mass function of $\bm{\beta}_{ij}$ at $\bm{\beta}^{(r)}$, i.e. $\theta_r = \mathbb{P}(\bm{\beta}_{ij} = \bm{\beta}^{(r)})$.
Then under the assumption $\tilde{\mathcal{B}} \approx \mathcal{B}$ we have that \eqref{eq:rcdcmconditionalprobability} boils down to
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FKRBpij}
    \mathbb{P}(y_{ij} = 1 \mid \bm{x}_{ij}) \approx \sum_{r = 1}^{R} \theta_r g_j\! \left(\bm{x_i},\bm{\beta}^{(r)}\right).
\end{equation}
We can then add $y_{ij}$ to both sides and move $\mathbb{P}(y_{ij}=1\mid \bm{x}_{ij})$ to the RHS:
\begin{align}
\label{eq:FKRBreg}
    y_{ij} &\approx \sum_{r = 1}^{R} \theta_r g_j\! \left(\bm{x_i},\bm{\beta}^{(r)}\right) + \left(y_{ij} - \mathbb{P}(y_{ij} = 1 \mid \bm{x}_{i})\right) \\
        &= \sum_{r = 1}^{R} \theta_r z_{ijr} + \zeta_{ij} = \bm{z}_{ij}^\prime \bm{\theta} + \zeta_{ij},
\end{align}
where $\bm{z}_{ij} = (z_{ij1}, z_{ij2}, \ldots, z_{ijR})$, with $z_{ijr} = g_j(\bm{x}_{i}, \bm{\beta}^{(r)})$ and $\zeta_{ij} = y_{ij} - \mathbb{P}(y_{ij} = 1 \mid \bm{x}_{ij})$.
It is important to note that all approximate equalities above are exact equalities in the case that $\tilde{\mathcal{B}} = \mathcal{B}$.

We notice that $\mathbb{E}(\zeta_{ij}) = 0$. Therefore, the OLS estimator
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FKRBOLSest}
    \hat{\bm{\theta}}^\mathrm{OLS} = \argmin_{\bm{\theta}}{\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{j=1}^{J}(y_{ij}-\bm{z}^\prime_{ij}\bm{\theta})^2} = (\bm{Z}'\bm{Z})^{-1}\bm{Z}'\bm{y},
\end{equation}
where $\bm{y} = (y_{11}, y_{12}, \ldots, y_{1J}, y_{21}, y_{22}, \ldots, y_{2J}, \ldots, y_{N1},\ldots, y_{NJ})$ and
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FKRBZdef}
    \bm{Z} = \begin{pmatrix}
    z_{111} & \cdots & z_{1J1} & z_{211} & \cdots & z_{2J1} & \cdots & z_{N11} & \cdots & z_{NJ1} \\
    z_{112} & \cdots & z_{1J2} & z_{212} & \cdots & z_{2J2} & \cdots & z_{N12} & \cdots & z_{NJ2} \\
    \vdots  &        & \vdots  & \vdots  &        & \vdots  &        & \vdots  &        & \vdots  \\
    z_{11R} & \cdots & z_{1JR} & z_{21R} & \cdots & z_{2JR} & \cdots & z_{N1R} & \cdots & z_{NJR}
    \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
is a consistent estimator for $\bm{\theta}$.\footnote{Look into how error independent from regressors condition is satisfied.}
However, we know $\bm{\theta}$ resembles a probability measure. 
This gives the following constraints:
\begin{align}
\label{eq:FKRBconstraint1}
    &\sum_{r=1}^{R}\theta_r = 1, \\
\label{eq:FKRBconstraint2}
    &\theta_r \geq 0, \text{ for all } r = 1,\ldots,R.
\end{align}
In practice one often wants to abide to these constraints. 
Therefore, \citet{FoxKimRyanBajari2011} suggest the following constrained least squares estimator
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FKRBCLSest}
    \begin{split}
        \hat{\bm{\theta}}^{\mathrm{FKRB}} = &\argmin_{\bm{\theta}}{\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{j=1}^{J}(y_{ij}-\bm{z}^\prime_{ij}\bm{\theta})^2}, \\
            &\text{ subject to \eqref{eq:FKRBconstraint1} and \eqref{eq:FKRBconstraint2}}.
    \end{split}
\end{equation}
Under some additional regularity conditions, this estimator is also consistent \citep{Liew1976}.
One then tries to reconstruct $F_{\bm{\beta}}$ according to
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FKRBest}
    \hat{F}_{\bm{\beta}}^{\mathrm{FKRB}}(\bm{b}) = \sum_{r=1}^R \left[\hat{\theta}^{\mathrm{FKRB}}_r \prod_{i=1}^{K}\mathds{1}{\{\beta^{r}_i < b_i\}}\right].
\end{equation}

We call this the FKRB estimator.\footnote{In the literature, \eqref{eq:FKRBCLSest} and \eqref{eq:FKRBest} are interchangeably called the FKRB estimator.}
The literature has provided some extensions to this estimator. 
\citet{FoxKimRyanBajari2011} themselves have provided a more efficient extension of the estimator.
It estimates $\bm{\theta}$ with generalized least squares, to account for the artificial heteroskedasticity in $\zeta_{ij}$.
\citet{HeissHetzeneckerOsterhaus2022} show that the FKRB estimator\footnote{Here in the sense of \eqref{eq:FKRBCLSest}.} is a special case of the nonnegative LASSO estimator.
They then generalize the FKRB estimator to a nonnegative elastic net estimator.
This estimator avoids randomly estimating many weights to zero; this behavior was commonly seen in both simulation and application studies on the FKRB estimator.

\subsection{The PSMD Estimator}
\label{sec:psmd}
We show that the FKRB estimator is also a special case of the PSMD estimator from \citet{ChenPouzo2012}.
Let
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:PSMDrho}
    \rho_{ij}(\bm{y},\bm{x};h) = y_{ij} - \int_{\mathcal{B}}g_j(\bm{x}_i,\bm{\beta})h(\bm{\beta})\,\mathrm{d}\bm{\beta},
\end{equation}
It follows that
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:PSMDexprho}
     \mathbb{E}[\rho_{ij}(\bm{y},\bm{x};h_0)\mid \bm{x}] = \mathbb{E}[y_{ij}\mid \bm{x}] - \int_{\mathcal{B}}g_j(\bm{x}_i,\bm{\beta})h_0(\bm{\beta})\,\mathrm{d}\bm{\beta} = 0
\end{equation}
for all $i,j$, under the identification $h_0(b) = \frac{\mathrm{d}F_{\bm{\beta}}(b)}{\mathrm{d}b}$, since
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:PSMDexpplink}
    \mathbb{E}[y_{ij}\mid \bm{x}] = \mathbb{E}[y_{ij}\mid \bm{x}_i] = \mathbb{P}(y_{ij}=1\mid \bm{x}_i) = \int_{\mathcal{B}}g_j(\bm{x}_{i},\bm{\beta})\,\mathrm{d}F_{\bm{\beta}} = \int_{\mathcal{B}}g_j(\bm{x}_i,\bm{\beta})h_0(\bm{\beta})\,\mathrm{d}\bm{\beta},
\end{equation}
by Proposition \ref{prop:rcdcmcondprop}.
This is exactly the framework in which \citet{ChenPouzo2012} provided the PSMD estimator for estimating $h_0$.

Define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:PSMDmdef}
    m_{ij}(\bm{x};h) \coloneq \mathbb{E}[\rho_{ij}(\bm{y},\bm{x};h)\mid \bm{x}].
\end{equation}
