\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Many econometric models of economic behavior allow for heterogeneity in individual responses to explanatory variables. 
This heterogeneity is often only captured in the error term.
Random coefficient models provide a more flexible and structural framework to capture heterogeneity, allowing parameters to vary across agents according to an unknown distribution. 
These models are widely used in labor economics, industrial organization, and asset pricing (e.g. \citet{BerryLevinsohnPakes1995}, \citet{CarneiroHeckmanVytlacil2011}, \citet{BelzilHansen2007} and \citet{KoijenYogo2019}). 
However, estimation of random coefficient models is challenging, especially when the model is nonparametric.

The Penalized Sieve Minimum Distance (PSMD) estimator provides a powerful framework for estimating such nonparametric models. 
PSMD combines sieve approximation, which allows infinite-dimensional parameters to be approximated by finite-dimensional spaces, with regularization, which stabilizes estimation in ill-posed problems. 
This framework is particularly useful when the parameter of interest lies in an infinite-dimensional function space, such as a distribution of random coefficients.

This thesis will study the performance of the PSMD estimator in random coefficient models, with a focus on its finite-sample behavior in a simulation of different data generating processes (DGPs). 
The goal is to understand when and how the PSMD estimator performs well, and to identify practical considerations for its implementation.
The performance of the PSMD estimator will also be compared to other more common estimators.
The thesis will focus on the results in a simulation setting, but when possible, it will also be attempted to back up some findings in the simulation with theoretical results.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}

In random coefficient models, we cannot estimate deterministic coefficients, as the coefficients are inherently random.
However, we can estimate the distribution of the random coefficients, giving us insight into the structure of the heterogeneity in the data.
One could do this parametrically, by assuming that the distribution of the random coefficients belongs to a specific family of distributions, such that the distributions in this family are identified by a finite-dimensional parameter.
This approach is quite common, but can only be justified if one has a strong reason to believe that the true distribution belongs to the chosen family.

The solution when no such strong prior belief exists is to take a nonparametric approach.
This approach is less restrictive, as it allows for a much wider range of possible distributions the estimator can choose from.
Indeed, in principle, every distribution could be estimated without bias in this approach.
However, nonparametric estimators often still restrict on the space of functions it can reach, to make the estimator computationaly feasible.

An example of such a nonparametric estimator are sieve estimators, which use an approximation of the infinite-dimensional distribution space by a sequence of (often finite-dimensional) smaller spaces.
This sequence is also referred to as a sieve \citep{Grenander1981}.
A specific example is the PSMD estimator.

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:literature}

The conceptual genesis of the methodology of sieves can be traced back to \citet{Grenander1981}.
Many papers on sieve estimation have since followed. These include \citet{GallantNychka1987}, who proposed an estimator that can be written as a sieve maximum likelihood estimator, \citet{WhiteWooldridge1991} and \citet{ChenShen1998}, who developed theory on sieve estimation in the context of dependent data, and \citet{Andrews1991} and \citet{Newey1997}, who developed much of the asymptotic theory on series estimators, which are to be seen as a specific type of sieve estimators.

An extensive overview of sieve estimation is given in \citet{Chen2007}.
This overview includes many examples of useful sieve classes, of which the class of Hermite polynomials spaces seems to be the most relevant to this thesis. 
This class allows for estimation of distributions that are defined over the whole real line.
However, if a random coefficient has some smaller support, other sieve classes might be more appropriate.
Furthermore, she also suggests some more creative nonlinear sieve classes, such as the class of neural network spaces, which might be an interesting class to explore as well.

\citet{AiChen2003} and \citet{NeweyPowell2003} laid the groundwork for the PSMD estimator by proposing a sieve minimum distance estimator (SMD). 
This has been extended to the PSMD estimator by \citet{ChenPouzo2012} by adding a penalty term. 
The usefulness of this estimator in nonparametric instrumental variable (NPIV) models is illustrated by \citet{ChenPouzo2015}.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

Random coefficient models are of the form
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rcgeneral}
    Y_i = g(\bm{X}_i, \bm{\beta}_i) + \varepsilon_i,
\end{equation}
for some known function $g: \mathbb{R}^d \times \mathbb{R}^k \to \mathbb{R}$, where $Y_i\in \mathbb{R}$ is the outcome variable, $\bm{X}_i\in \mathbb{R}^d$ is a $d\times 1$-vector of regressors, $\bm{\beta}_i \in \mathbb{R}^k$ a $k \times 1$-vector of random coefficients, such that $\bm{\beta}_i \sim F_{\bm{\beta}}$ for some unknown distribution $F_{\bm{\beta}}$, and $\varepsilon_i \in \mathbb{R}$ is a stochastic error term.
Common examples for $g$ include the linear model
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rclinear}
    Y_i = \bm{X}_i^\prime \bm{\beta}_i + \varepsilon_i,
\end{equation}
and the random coefficient logit model
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rclogit}
    Y_i = \mathds{1}\{\bm{X}_i^\prime \bm{\beta}_i + \varepsilon_i \geq 0\}.
\end{equation}

The goal is to estimate the distribution of $\bm{\beta}$, denoted by $F_{\bm{\beta}}$. 
This problem is inherently infinite-dimensional and often ill-posed, particularly when the model can be written as a conditional moment restriction:
\[
\mathbb{E}[\rho(Z_i, \theta_0) \mid W_i] = 0,
\]
where $\theta_0$ is an infinite-dimensional parameter.

The PSMD estimator approximates $\theta_0$ using sieve spaces and imposes a penalty term to ensure stability and regularization. 
However, the finite-sample performance depends heavily on tuning parameters, sieve dimension, penalty choice, and properties of the DGP. 
These issues are not yet fully understood in the context of random coefficient models.
